# Laws of UX

Source: lawsofux.com — 30 psychological principles for interface design.

## Heuristic Laws (Decision-Making)

### 1. Aesthetic-Usability Effect
Users perceive aesthetically pleasing design as more usable. Visual polish isn't vanity — it directly increases perceived functionality and user trust. Invest in visual quality; it buys forgiveness for minor usability issues.

### 2. Hick's Law
Decision time increases with the number and complexity of choices. Reduce options systematically. Present choices hierarchically. Break complex decisions into smaller sequential steps.

### 3. Miller's Law
Working memory holds 7 +/- 2 items. Limit lists, menus, and visible options to 5-9 items. Chunk related information into meaningful groups.

### 4. Fitts's Law
Time to acquire a target = f(distance, size). Make primary actions large and close to the user's current focus. Reduce distance between sequential actions. Touch targets >= 44px.

### 5. Jakob's Law
Users spend most time on OTHER sites. They expect yours to work the same way. Follow platform conventions. Use familiar patterns. Innovate on value, not on navigation.

### 6. Occam's Razor
The simplest solution that works is the best. Eliminate unnecessary complexity. Every element must earn its place.

### 7. Pareto Principle (80/20)
80% of effects come from 20% of causes. Focus design effort on the 20% of features that deliver 80% of user value.

## Perception Laws (How Users See)

### 8. Law of Proximity
Objects near each other are perceived as grouped. Use spacing to create visual relationships. Related items close; unrelated items far.

### 9. Law of Similarity
Similar elements are perceived as related. Consistent styling (color, shape, size) signals "these belong together" across the interface.

### 10. Law of Common Region
Elements sharing a bounded area are perceived as grouped. Use containers, cards, and backgrounds to delineate groups.

### 11. Law of Uniform Connectedness
Visually connected elements are perceived as more related. Lines, shared backgrounds, and enclosures strengthen perceived associations.

### 12. Law of Pragnanz
Users interpret complex visuals as the simplest possible form. Simplify visual designs — users will find the simplest interpretation anyway.

### 13. Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)
The item that differs from the rest is most likely remembered. Use visual differentiation for primary CTAs, warnings, and key information.

### 14. Serial Position Effect
Users remember first and last items best. Place critical content at the beginning and end of sequences, lists, and navigation.

## Cognitive Laws (How Users Think)

### 15. Cognitive Load
The total mental resources needed to understand and interact. Minimize extraneous load. Keep intrinsic load appropriate. Maximize germane load (actual learning/doing).

### 16. Choice Overload
Too many options causes paralysis and regret. Curate options. Use progressive disclosure. Default to the best choice.

### 17. Chunking
Group individual pieces into meaningful wholes. Phone numbers, credit cards, addresses — all chunked for human memory.

### 18. Mental Model
Users carry compressed models of how systems work. Design must match these models or explicitly teach new ones. Never assume your model matches theirs.

### 19. Selective Attention
Users focus on stimuli related to their current goal. Use contrast, motion, and hierarchy to guide attention to goal-relevant elements.

### 20. Working Memory
Temporary storage for active tasks. Reduce demands — show context, provide undo, never require users to remember across screens.

### 21. Cognitive Bias
Systematic errors in judgment. Design should account for anchoring, confirmation bias, loss aversion. Provide balanced information.

## Behavioral Laws (How Users Act)

### 22. Doherty Threshold
Productivity soars at <400ms response time. Keep interactions under 400ms. Show progress for longer operations. Never leave users waiting without feedback.

### 23. Goal-Gradient Effect
Effort increases as users approach their goal. Show progress. Make completion visible. The closer to done, the more motivated they become.

### 24. Peak-End Rule
Experiences are judged by their peak moment and ending. Optimize the highest-impact moment and the final interaction. A great ending forgives a rough middle.

### 25. Zeigarnik Effect
Unfinished tasks are remembered better than completed ones. Use progress indicators. Incomplete states drive users back to finish.

### 26. Flow
Full immersion in a task. Balance challenge with skill. Remove distractions. Provide clear goals and immediate feedback.

### 27. Paradox of the Active User
Users never read manuals. They start doing immediately. Enable immediate exploration. Make interfaces self-evident.

### 28. Parkinson's Law
Work expands to fill available time. Set constraints. Deadlines prevent scope creep. Time pressure can improve focus.

## System Laws (How Systems Should Behave)

### 29. Postel's Law
Be liberal in what you accept, conservative in what you send. Accept varied inputs gracefully. Output with precision and consistency.

### 30. Tesler's Law (Conservation of Complexity)
Every system has irreducible complexity. Decide who bears it — the user or the system. The system should absorb as much complexity as possible.
